# SEC — Problematic Practices Under Regulation S (Release 33-7190)

## Source Summary

SEC interpretive release issued in June 1995 publicly identifying abusive practices the SEC had been seeing in Reg S equity offerings since 1990 — and warning that the safe harbor would not protect them. This release is the foundational document driving every subsequent Reg S amendment.

## What It Contributes

### Schemes Flagged
- **Sham offshore closes**: U.S. issuer "sells" to a foreign nominee who immediately resells back into U.S. markets after the (then) 40-day restricted period — the offshore party never bore real risk
- **Pre-arranged U.S. flowback**: agreements at closing to push securities back to specified U.S. buyers
- **Hedging during the restricted period**: offshore buyer enters short positions in the U.S. on day 1 to lock in the resale spread
- **Discount magnitude as red flag**: deeply discounted offshore placements in non-trading-volume situations are presumptive evidence of intent to flow back

### SEC's Test
The SEC said it would look to substance over form. If the economic reality is that the securities were destined for U.S. markets from the start, the offering loses Reg S protection no matter how the documents read.

### Long-Term Impact
This release led directly to the 1998 amendments (Release 33-7505) which:
- Added Rule 905 (no wash-off — see [[Pillsbury-Restricted-Securities-Reg-S]])
- Lengthened the U.S. equity distribution compliance period from 40 days to 1 year
- Added hedging restrictions for Cat 3 equity offerings
- Tightened buyer certification and transfer legend requirements

## Why High Confidence

This is an SEC interpretive release — primary regulatory text. The 1995 abuses it described led to durable amendments still in force in 2026.
