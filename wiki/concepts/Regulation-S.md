# Regulation S — Offshore Offerings of Securities

Regulation S is a safe harbor under the U.S. Securities Act of 1933 that permits offers and sales of securities **outside the United States** without registering them with the SEC. Adopted in 1990, it codified the SEC's territorial approach: U.S. registration applies to U.S. capital markets, not to genuine offshore transactions.

It is *not* an exemption — it is a safe harbor under Section 5. Compliance gives certainty that an offering falls outside U.S. registration; non-compliance does not automatically destroy the offering, but the issuer must then prove on first principles that no U.S. offer or sale occurred.

## The Two Core Conditions (Rule 903 / Rule 904)

Every Reg S offering must satisfy two conditions:

1. **Offshore transaction** — The offer is not made to a person *in* the United States, AND either (a) the buyer is reasonably believed to be outside the U.S. when the buy order originates, OR (b) the trade executes on an established foreign securities exchange or designated offshore market. (Rule 902(h)) (Source: [[Cornell-LII-Rule-902-Definitions]])
2. **No directed selling efforts in the U.S.** — No activity intended to, or that could reasonably be expected to, condition the U.S. market for the securities. (Rule 902(c)) (Source: [[Cleary-Reg-S-Selling-Transfer-Restrictions]])

These conditions are non-negotiable. Failing either destroys the safe harbor immediately. (Source: [[Fourscore-Regulation-S-Guide]])

## The Five Rules of Reg S

| Rule | Function |
|------|----------|
| **901** | General statement: territorial scope of Section 5 |
| **902** | Definitions (US person, offshore transaction, directed selling efforts, distribution compliance period, SUSMI, etc.) |
| **903** | Issuer / distributor / affiliate safe harbor (the issuance side) |
| **904** | Resale safe harbor (the secondary-market side) |
| **905** | Resale of equity securities of U.S. domestic issuers — preserves "restricted security" status under Rule 144 |

## Three Categories — Different Restrictions for Different Risk

Reg S sorts offerings into three categories based on **(a) where the issuer reports**, **(b) whether there is Substantial U.S. Market Interest (SUSMI)**, and **(c) equity vs debt**. Higher U.S.-flowback risk → stricter rules. (Source: [[Cleary-Reg-S-Selling-Transfer-Restrictions]])

### Category 1 — Lowest Risk
- Issuer has **no SUSMI** in the security class, OR
- Foreign government securities, OR
- Securities offered to employees of an offshore branch under a foreign benefit plan, OR
- Overseas directed offerings into a single foreign country

**Restrictions:** Only the two core conditions (offshore transaction + no directed selling efforts). No distribution compliance period.

### Category 2 — Medium Risk
- Equity securities of an Exchange Act **reporting foreign issuer**, OR
- Debt securities of any Exchange Act reporting issuer, OR
- Debt securities of a non-reporting foreign issuer

**Restrictions:**
- 40-day **distribution compliance period** during which sales to U.S. persons are blocked
- Offering restrictions: distributor agreements, transfer restriction legends, confirmation notices

### Category 3 — Highest Risk (catch-all)
- Everything not in Cat 1 or Cat 2 — most importantly, **equity of non-reporting U.S. issuers** and **equity of foreign issuers with SUSMI but not reporting under the Exchange Act**

**Distribution compliance periods:**
- Equity of U.S. issuer: **1 year**
- Equity of foreign issuer: **6 months**
- Debt securities: **40 days**

Plus stricter offering restrictions, certifications from buyers, mandatory transfer legends, and (for equity) hedging restrictions.

> [!gap] The 1-year period for U.S. equity issuers is the key trap. Most U.S. private companies raising capital fall into Cat 3.

## Defined Terms That Decide Everything

### "U.S. Person" (Rule 902(k))
Eight categories. Practical shorthand: anyone or any entity tied to the U.S. by residence, formation, or fiduciary control. Specifically includes:
1. Natural person resident in the U.S.
2. Partnership or corporation organized under U.S. law
3. Estate with a U.S. executor/administrator
4. Trust with a U.S. trustee
5. Agency or branch of a foreign entity *located in* the U.S.
6. Non-discretionary fiduciary account benefiting a U.S. person
7. Discretionary account held by a U.S. fiduciary
8. Foreign entity formed by a U.S. person principally to invest in unregistered securities (with carve-outs for accredited entities)

**Carve-outs**: a foreign professional fiduciary acting for a U.S. client is *not* a U.S. person; an offshore branch of a U.S. bank/broker-dealer acting in agency for a non-U.S. client may not be either. (Source: [[Cornell-LII-Rule-902-Definitions]])

### "Directed Selling Efforts" (Rule 902(c))
Any activity that conditions the U.S. market for the Reg S securities. Captures:
- Ads in U.S. publications, U.S. radio/TV
- Promotional seminars in the U.S.
- Direct contact with persons known to be in the U.S.

**Permitted (not directed selling efforts):**
- Tombstone ads required by foreign or U.S. law (with disclaimer)
- Offering documents delivered to QIBs under Rule 144A
- Routine activity not designed to condition the U.S. market

A single careless email blast can blow the whole safe harbor. (Source: [[Cleary-Reg-S-Selling-Transfer-Restrictions]])

### "Substantial U.S. Market Interest" (SUSMI) — Rule 902(j)
The test that decides Cat 1 vs Cat 2/3:
- **Equity SUSMI**: in the prior year, U.S. exchanges/ATSs were the single largest market for that class, OR ≥20% of trading occurred in the U.S. and < a threshold offshore.
- **Debt SUSMI**: > 300 U.S. record holders, > 20% held by U.S. persons, > $1B principal amount.

If no SUSMI → Cat 1 (lightest restrictions).

### "Distribution Compliance Period"
The post-closing window during which Reg S buyers cannot resell to U.S. persons (other than under another exemption). Replaces what was historically called the "restricted period." Length depends on category and security type (40 days / 6 months / 1 year).

## Rule 905 — The Wash-Off Block

Equity securities of a **U.S. domestic issuer** placed offshore under Reg S remain **"restricted securities" under Rule 144** even if resold abroad. They cannot be "washed clean" by repeated offshore resales. The Rule 144 holding period (typically 6 months for reporting issuers, 1 year for non-reporting) governs when they can flow back into the U.S. (Source: [[Pillsbury-Restricted-Securities-Reg-S]])

This is the single most-misunderstood rule. Issuers who think Reg S = freely tradable abroad → "freely re-importable" are wrong, and have been the SEC's enforcement target since 1995. (Source: [[SEC-Problematic-Practices-Reg-S-1995]])

## Who Uses Reg S

- **Foreign issuers raising in their home market** without SEC registration
- **U.S. issuers raising offshore** — typically Eurobond debt, or international tranches alongside a U.S. private placement
- **Combined Rule 144A / Reg S offerings** — global debt deals that simultaneously sell to U.S. QIBs (144A) and offshore buyers (Reg S); see [[Rule-144A]]
- **Crypto / digital asset offerings** marketed to non-U.S. participants (often paired with Reg D for U.S. accredited investors)

## Common Failure Modes

1. **U.S. roadshow slips**. Investor relations meets U.S. portfolio managers ahead of pricing → directed selling efforts.
2. **Press release distributed via U.S. wire services**. Reads in NY → conditioning the U.S. market.
3. **U.S. broker pre-marketing without 144A wrapper**. Need a 144A leg or no U.S.-side activity.
4. **Equity flowback inside distribution compliance period**. Cat 3 hedge funds buying offshore and reselling to U.S. retail in week 6.
5. **Sham offshore close**. Booking the trade in London with a U.S. counterparty already pre-arranged → SEC views this as a U.S. transaction; safe harbor lost. (Source: [[SEC-Problematic-Practices-Reg-S-1995]])

## Reg S vs Reg D — Choose the Buyer Pool

| | Reg D (Rule 506) | Reg S |
|---|---|---|
| Buyer | U.S. accredited investors | Non-U.S. persons |
| Geography | U.S. focus, general solicitation under 506(c) | Offshore only, no U.S. solicitation |
| Resale | Restricted securities, Rule 144 | Distribution compliance period + Rule 905 if U.S. issuer |
| Reporting | Form D filed with SEC | No SEC filing |
| Combined? | Frequently used together — Reg D for U.S. accredited investors + Reg S for offshore tranche, treated as separate offerings if integration safe harbors apply |

## Crypto / Token Context (2025–2026)

Token offerings increasingly use a Reg D + Reg S structure: Reg D for U.S. accredited buyers, Reg S for the global tranche. SAFTs (Simple Agreements for Future Tokens) are the typical instrument. Post-2025 SEC posture under Chairman Atkins is meaningfully friendlier to digital-asset issuers, but the Reg S core conditions are unchanged. (Source: [[Cleary-2026-Digital-Assets-Update]] — see [[Research: US Reg S]] for context)

## Practical Checklist for an Issuer

1. Identify SUSMI status → which Category applies?
2. Lock distribution compliance period and put procedures in place to enforce it
3. Add Reg S transfer legend to certificates / token wrappers
4. Distributor agreements with all selling agents committing to no directed selling efforts
5. Buyer certifications (non-U.S. person; offshore at time of order)
6. If raising in U.S. concurrently → structure as Reg D / 144A / Reg S three-leg, treat them as separate offerings, isolate marketing channels
7. For U.S. domestic issuers: account for Rule 905 — these are restricted securities for Rule 144 purposes
