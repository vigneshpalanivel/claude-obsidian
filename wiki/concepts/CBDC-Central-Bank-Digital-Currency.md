# Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs)

A CBDC is a digital form of a country's sovereign currency, issued and controlled by the central bank. Unlike cryptocurrency, it is centralized and represents a direct liability of the central bank.

## Global Adoption Status (2025-2026)

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Countries exploring CBDC | 137 (representing 98% of global GDP) |
| Active CBDC pilot projects | 49 (new high) |
| Fully launched CBDCs | 3 (Bahamas, Jamaica, Nigeria) |

## Regional Leaders

### China — e-CNY (Digital Yuan)
- Furthest advanced major economy CBDC
- 2.25 billion digital wallets created
- Still technically in "pilot" phase but operationally deployed at scale

### India — e-Rupee
- Second-largest CBDC pilot globally
- Circulation: ₹10.16 billion ($122M) by March 2025
- 334% year-over-year growth (from $28M in 2024)

### Europe — Digital Euro
- ECB in 2-year preparation phase (not yet launched)
- Bank of England evaluating digital pound
- Swiss National Bank: wholesale CBDC pilot active

### Hong Kong — e-HKD
- Pilot programme completed October 2025
- Preparatory work for potential extension: targeting H1 2026

### United States — No CBDC
- President Trump issued executive order prohibiting establishment, issuance, and use of a US CBDC
- US path is via regulated stablecoins (GENIUS Act) rather than government CBDC

## CBDC vs. Stablecoins vs. Cryptocurrency

| Dimension | CBDC | Stablecoin | Cryptocurrency |
|-----------|------|-----------|---------------|
| Issuer | Central bank | Private company | Decentralized protocol |
| Backing | Sovereign guarantee | Cash/reserves | Algorithm/market |
| Control | Centralized | Centralized (mostly) | Decentralized |
| Blockchain needed? | No (can use DLT or not) | Yes | Yes |
| Legal tender | Yes | No | No |

> BIS note: CBDCs would be centrally controlled even if implemented on a distributed database; a traditional blockchain is likely not required or useful for most CBDC architectures.

## Fintech Implications

**Threat to fintech**: If central banks issue retail CBDCs directly to consumers, intermediary fintech layers (neobanks, payment processors) could be disintermediated.

**Opportunity for fintech**: Most CBDC models use a "two-tier" approach where central banks issue to commercial banks, which distribute to consumers. Fintech infrastructure providers become critical distribution rails.

**Wholesale CBDC** (bank-to-bank): Lower political risk, more technically tractable; Swiss National Bank, BIS Project Nexus and Jura are wholesale models.

## Open Questions

> [!gap] Retail CBDC adoption in the three fully-launched countries (Bahamas, Jamaica, Nigeria) has been low despite availability. Root causes — UX, merchant acceptance, trust — not fully documented.

> [!gap] How the US "no CBDC" policy interacts with dollar stablecoin dominance globally is unresolved. Could regulated USD stablecoins effectively function as a de facto digital dollar?
