# Regulation S — Offshore Securities Offering Compliance Checklist
## The Legal Advisor's First-Meeting Guide for Offshore Securities Offerings Outside the United States

**Regulation:** U.S. Regulation S — 17 CFR §§ 230.901–230.905 (Securities Act of 1933)
**Issuing Authority:** U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)
**Status:** In force (as of eCFR update 6 June 2026)
**Document type:** Pre-offering readiness assessment — to be completed before any offshore securities offering commences

---

> **Who this document is for:** Founders, issuers, underwriters, and legal teams planning to offer or sell securities outside the United States and seeking to avoid U.S. Securities Act Section 5 registration by relying on the Regulation S offshore safe harbor. This checklist mirrors the intake assessment a U.S. securities lawyer would walk you through before structuring an offshore offering.

---

> **How to use this checklist:** Work through each section in order. Every item marked with a checkbox (☐) represents a concrete requirement or decision you must resolve. Items you cannot check off represent gaps that close the safe harbor — meaning you may need U.S. registration or another exemption. Your legal advisor will use your responses to scope the engagement, select the correct offering category, and identify disqualifying facts early.

---

## SECTION 1 — DOES REGULATION S APPLY TO YOU?

*Before invoking Regulation S, confirm you are in the right regulatory framework. Reg S is a safe harbor from Section 5 registration — not an exemption from fraud liability or reporting obligations.*

---

### 1.1 — Threshold Questions: Is Section 5 Registration a Concern?

Answer each question. If any answer is **Yes**, Section 5 of the Securities Act may apply — and Regulation S may be the safe harbor you need.

- [ ] Are you offering or selling a "security" as defined under U.S. law (including investment contracts under the *Howey* test, notes, bonds, equity interests, or digital asset tokens that qualify as securities)?
- [ ] Is your offering conducted in whole or in part outside the United States?
- [ ] Are you attempting to avoid U.S. registration while distributing to non-U.S. investors?
- [ ] Are you an issuer, distributor, or affiliate seeking to structure sales so they fall outside Section 5's reach?
- [ ] Are you a secondary market seller seeking to resell securities originally issued offshore?

> **Legal advisor note:** Regulation S provides a safe harbor only from Section 5 (registration). It does not exempt you from anti-fraud provisions (Section 17(a) of the Securities Act, Section 10(b) of the Exchange Act), the reporting obligations of Exchange Act-registered issuers, or state (blue sky) laws. Securities counsel must separately assess fraud exposure and reporting status.

**Reference:** 17 CFR § 230.901; Securities Act § 5 (15 U.S.C. § 77e)

---

### 1.2 — Scope of the Safe Harbor: What Regulation S Covers

Regulation S deems certain offers and sales NOT to occur within the United States for purposes of Section 5 — eliminating the registration requirement for those transactions.

- [ ] You understand that Regulation S does **not** exempt securities from ongoing SEC reporting (Exchange Act) if the issuer is already a reporting company
- [ ] You understand that Regulation S does **not** create an exemption from broker-dealer registration requirements for intermediaries
- [ ] You understand that equity securities of **domestic issuers** acquired in Reg S transactions are **always "restricted securities"** under Rule 144, regardless of the offshore nature of the original sale (Rule 905)
- [ ] You understand that Reg S is a **safe harbor**, not a statutory exemption — if the conditions are not met, Section 5 registration is required or another exemption must be found

> **Legal advisor note:** Issuers who improperly condition the U.S. market during a Reg S offering (through directed selling efforts or inadequate distribution compliance periods) face the full registration and rescission risk of Section 5 violations. The SEC has brought enforcement actions against issuers who used Reg S as a backdoor to U.S. distribution.

**Reference:** 17 CFR §§ 230.901–230.905

---

## SECTION 2 — KEY DEFINITIONS YOU MUST UNDERSTAND (Rule 902)

*Regulation S is a definitions-heavy regulation. Misunderstanding even one term — especially "U.S. person," "offshore transaction," or "directed selling efforts" — collapses the safe harbor. Work through this section before proceeding.*

---

### 2.1 — Who Is a "U.S. Person"? (Rule 902(k))

The following ARE "U.S. persons" — sales to them during the distribution compliance period are prohibited under Category 2 and 3:

- [ ] Any **natural person resident in the United States**
- [ ] Any **partnership or corporation organized or incorporated under U.S. law**
- [ ] Any **estate** of which any executor or administrator is a U.S. person
- [ ] Any **trust** of which any trustee is a U.S. person
- [ ] Any **agency or branch of a foreign entity located in the United States**
- [ ] Any **non-discretionary account** held by a dealer or fiduciary **for the benefit of a U.S. person**
- [ ] Any **discretionary account** held by a dealer or fiduciary organized, incorporated, or (if an individual) resident in the United States
- [ ] Any partnership or corporation organized under foreign law but **formed by a U.S. person principally to invest in unregistered securities** — unless the entity is **both organized and owned** by accredited investors (as defined in Rule 501(a)) who are **not natural persons, estates, or trusts** (i.e., institutional accredited investors only)

The following are **NOT "U.S. persons"** and sales to them are permissible:

- [ ] Discretionary accounts held **for the benefit of a non-U.S. person** by a U.S.-resident dealer or fiduciary
- [ ] Estates where a **non-U.S. executor has sole or shared investment discretion** and the estate is governed by foreign law
- [ ] Trusts where a **non-U.S. trustee has sole or shared investment discretion** and no beneficiary or settlor is a U.S. person
- [ ] **Employee benefit plans** established and administered under foreign law
- [ ] **Foreign branches of U.S. persons** that operate for valid business reasons and are engaged in insurance/banking subject to local regulation
- [ ] **International organizations** (IMF, World Bank, UN, Asian Development Bank, African Development Bank, Inter-American Development Bank, their affiliates and pension plans)

> **Legal advisor note:** The U.S. person definition is deliberately broad. "Resident in the United States" is facts-and-circumstances based — a non-U.S. national temporarily in the U.S. may qualify. For any offering marketed internationally through electronic channels, geofencing alone is insufficient — you need layered controls including IP restriction, self-certification, KYC, and purchase-agreement representations.

**Reference:** 17 CFR § 230.902(k)

---

### 2.2 — What Is an "Offshore Transaction"? (Rule 902(h))

An offer or sale is made in an "offshore transaction" **only if both of the following are true**:

- [ ] **The offer is not made to a person in the United States**; AND
- [ ] **Either:**
  - At the time the buy order is originated, the buyer is **outside the United States** (or the seller reasonably believes so); OR
  - For Rule 903 purposes: the transaction is executed **on a physical trading floor of an established foreign securities exchange** located outside the U.S.; OR
  - For Rule 904 purposes: the transaction is executed through a **designated offshore securities market** (Rule 902(b)) and **neither the seller nor their agent knows** the transaction was pre-arranged with a U.S. buyer

The following do **NOT** qualify as offshore transactions:

- [ ] Offers and sales **specifically targeted at identifiable groups of U.S. citizens abroad** (e.g., U.S. armed forces personnel serving overseas) — these are treated as U.S. offers regardless of physical location

> **Legal advisor note:** "The offer is not made to a person in the United States" is interpreted strictly. Online platforms and websites accessible to U.S. residents require active blocking, not just disclaimers. Sending solicitation emails to U.S. email addresses — even if the recipient happens to be abroad — may fail this test.

**Reference:** 17 CFR § 230.902(h)

---

### 2.3 — What Are "Directed Selling Efforts"? (Rule 902(c))

"Directed selling efforts" means **any activity** undertaken for the purpose of, or reasonably expected to have the effect of, **conditioning the market in the United States** for securities offered under Regulation S. This includes:

- [ ] Placing an advertisement in a publication **with general circulation in the United States** referring to the Reg S offering. Rule 902(c)(2)(i) defines this as a publication that **either**: (a) is **printed primarily for distribution in the United States**; or (b) has had, during the preceding 12 months, an **average U.S. circulation of 15,000+ copies per issue**. (Per Rule 902(c)(2)(ii), only the U.S. edition counts where a publication prints a separate U.S. edition and the non-U.S. base publication would not itself qualify)
- [ ] Any marketing activity targeted at U.S. investors or the U.S. market generally

The following are **NOT** "directed selling efforts":

- [ ] Legally required advertisements that contain only required information plus a statement that securities are unregistered and may not be offered in the U.S. absent registration or exemption
- [ ] Contact with persons excluded from the U.S. person definition (Rule 902(k)(2)(vi)) solely in their capacity as account holders
- [ ] **Tombstone advertisements** in publications where U.S. circulation is less than 20% of total combined circulation, if the ad contains a legend about U.S. registration status and only limited factual information (issuer name, security title, business description, price, yield, underwriter names, dates, rights offering terms, required legends)
- [ ] Bona fide visits to real estate, plants, or facilities in the U.S. by prospective investors
- [ ] Distribution of foreign broker-dealer quotations through third-party systems that operate primarily in foreign countries, if transactions cannot be executed through the system between foreign dealers and U.S. persons
- [ ] Rule 135 or Rule 135c notices
- [ ] Press access to offshore press conferences or materials under Rule 135e conditions
- [ ] Research reports published by a broker or dealer under Rule 138(c) or Rule 139(b)

> **Legal advisor note:** Websites, social media accounts, email distribution lists, and any other electronic channels accessible to U.S. users are the highest-risk areas for inadvertent directed selling efforts. A single public post referencing the offering that is readable in the U.S. may suffice. Geo-restricted content and platform-level IP blocking are minimum hygiene, not comprehensive solutions.

**Reference:** 17 CFR § 230.902(c)

---

### 2.4 — Other Critical Definitions

- [ ] **Distributor (Rule 902(d)):** Any underwriter, dealer, or other person participating pursuant to a contractual arrangement in the distribution of Reg S securities. Distributors have different obligations than ordinary investors — confirm who in your structure qualifies
- [ ] **Distribution compliance period (Rule 902(f)):** Begins when securities are first offered to persons other than distributors OR at closing (whichever is later); ends at the applicable category deadline. In **continuous offerings**, it commences at completion of distribution, as certified by the managing underwriter
- [ ] **Offering restrictions (Rule 902(g)):** Written distributor agreement plus offering materials must include the required legend (securities unregistered, may not be sold in U.S. or to U.S. persons absent registration or exemption). For equity of domestic issuers, must also include hedging restriction language
- [ ] **Reporting issuer (Rule 902(i)):** An issuer with a class of securities registered under Exchange Act Section 12(b) or 12(g), or required to file under Section 15(d), that has filed all required reports for at least 12 months. Reporting-issuer status shortens the Category 3 equity compliance period from 1 year to 6 months
- [ ] **Substantial U.S. market interest — equity (Rule 902(j)(1)):** U.S. markets constitute the single largest market for the equity class, OR ≥20% of trading is in the U.S. and <55% is in any single foreign country
- [ ] **Substantial U.S. market interest — debt (Rule 902(j)(2)):** 300+ U.S. record holders; ≥$1 billion of principal outstanding (or liquidation preference / par value for non-participatory preferred or asset-backed securities) held by U.S. persons; ≥20% of principal outstanding held by U.S. persons. The regulation lists these as (i), (ii), (iii); the SEC's adopting release and standard practice read all three as **conjunctive** (all must be met). Confirm current SEC guidance with counsel before relying on a no-SUSMI determination for debt
- [ ] **Designated offshore securities market (Rule 902(b)):** Rule 902(b)(1) enumerates 28 markets — the Eurobond market (as regulated by ISMA) plus 27 exchanges (Alberta, Amsterdam, Australian, Bermuda, Brussels, Copenhagen, EASDAQ, Frankfurt, Helsinki, Hong Kong, Irish, Istanbul, Johannesburg, London, Luxembourg, Mexico, Milan, Montreal, Oslo, Paris, Singapore, Stockholm, Tokyo, Toronto, Vancouver, Warsaw, Zurich). Rule 902(b)(2) permits the SEC to designate additional markets based on enumerated attributes (foreign-law organization, oversight, reporting, clearance/settlement systems)

**Reference:** 17 CFR § 230.902

## SECTION 3 — WHICH OFFERING CATEGORY APPLIES TO YOU? (Rule 903)

*Rule 903 covers offers and sales by the issuer, distributors, their affiliates, and persons acting on their behalf. You must satisfy the universal conditions PLUS the category-specific conditions. The category is determined by who the issuer is and what kind of securities are being sold.*

---

### 3.1 — Universal Conditions (All Categories, Rule 903(a))

All Reg S issuer offerings must satisfy ALL THREE of the following:

- [ ] **The offer or sale is made in an offshore transaction** (Rule 902(h) — Section 2.2 above)
- [ ] **No directed selling efforts are made in the United States** by the issuer, any distributor, any affiliate, or any person acting on their behalf (Rule 902(c) — Section 2.3 above)
- [ ] **The applicable category-specific conditions** (Category 1, 2, or 3 below) are satisfied

---

### 3.2 — Determine Your Category

Work through the decision tree below. The first category for which you qualify is your applicable category.

#### ☐ CATEGORY 1 — No Additional Conditions (Rule 903(b)(1))

*Category 1 is the most favorable — only the universal conditions apply. No distribution compliance period, no offering restrictions, no U.S.-person exclusion required.*

Securities qualify for Category 1 if **any one** of the following applies:

- [ ] **Foreign issuer / no substantial U.S. market interest:** Securities issued by a **foreign issuer** that reasonably believes at commencement of the offering that:
  - No substantial U.S. market interest in the equity class (if offering equity)
  - No substantial U.S. market interest in its debt securities (if offering debt)
  - No substantial U.S. market interest in the securities to be purchased on exercise (if offering warrants)
  - No substantial U.S. market interest in either the convertible securities or underlying securities (if offering convertibles)

- [ ] **Overseas directed offering:** Securities offered and sold in an overseas directed offering (directed into a **single country other than the U.S.**, in accordance with local law and customary practices):
  - **Foreign issuer** offering any type of security directed into a single foreign country; OR
  - **Domestic issuer** offering **non-convertible debt** directed into a single foreign country **to residents thereof**, **made in accordance with the local laws and customary practices and documentation of that country**, where principal/interest (or par value) is denominated in a currency other than U.S. dollars, not convertible into USD-denominated securities, and not linked to USD in a manner that in effect converts the securities to USD-denominated securities — **except** through related currency or interest rate **swap transactions that are commercial in nature** (a carve-out for legitimate hedging)

- [ ] **Foreign government backing:** Securities backed by the **full faith and credit of a foreign government**

- [ ] **Foreign employee benefit plan:** Securities offered and sold to employees of the issuer or its affiliates pursuant to an **employee benefit plan** established and administered under foreign law, provided:
  - [ ] Securities are issued as compensation for bona fide services not rendered in connection with a capital-raising transaction
  - [ ] Plan interests are not transferable other than by will or laws of descent
  - [ ] Issuer takes reasonable steps to preclude offers and sales to U.S. residents (other than employees on temporary U.S. assignment)
  - [ ] Offering documentation contains a legend that securities are unregistered and may not be offered in the U.S. absent registration or exemption

> **Legal advisor note:** Category 1 is commonly relied on by established foreign private issuers with no substantial U.S. market interest. The "no substantial U.S. market interest" determination must be made reasonably and at the commencement of the offering — document the factual basis (market-share data, holder analysis, trading-venue breakdown) in a memorandum contemporaneous with the offering launch.

**Reference:** 17 CFR § 230.903(b)(1)

---

#### ☐ CATEGORY 2 — 40-Day Distribution Compliance Period (Rule 903(b)(2))

*Category 2 applies to securities not eligible for Category 1 that are: (i) equity securities of a reporting foreign issuer, or (ii) debt securities of a reporting issuer (U.S. or foreign) or of a non-reporting foreign issuer.*

Conditions that must be satisfied:

- [ ] **Offering restrictions implemented** — each distributor has agreed in writing that all pre-compliance-period offers and sales comply with Reg S, or are registered/exempt; offering materials include the required unregistered-securities legend on the cover, in the underwriting section, and in all advertisements (Rule 902(g))
- [ ] **No U.S.-person sales prior to 40-day distribution compliance period expiry** — offers and sales made before the compliance period expires are not made to a U.S. person or for the account or benefit of a U.S. person (other than a distributor)
- [ ] **Dealer notification** — each distributor selling to a dealer or person receiving a selling concession, prior to expiry of the 40-day period, sends a **confirmation or other notice** to the purchaser stating that the purchaser is subject to the same restrictions on offers and sales that apply to a distributor

> **Legal advisor note:** Category 2 is the standard category for investment-grade debt offerings by foreign issuers and reporting-company debt. The 40-day distribution compliance period aligns with the Regulation S distribution restriction and the typical global offering settlement timeline.

**Reference:** 17 CFR § 230.903(b)(2)

---

#### ☐ CATEGORY 3 — Enhanced Conditions (Rule 903(b)(3))

*Category 3 is the most restrictive. It applies to all securities not eligible for Category 1 or 2 — typically equity securities of domestic issuers and debt securities of non-reporting domestic issuers.*

**Universal Category 3 conditions:**

- [ ] **Offering restrictions implemented** (same as Category 2, including hedging restriction for domestic-issuer equity)
- [ ] **Dealer notification** — each distributor selling to a dealer or selling-concession recipient, prior to the applicable compliance period expiry, sends a confirmation or notice stating the restrictions

**For debt securities:**

- [ ] Offers and sales prior to expiry of the **40-day distribution compliance period** are not made to a U.S. person or for U.S. account/benefit (other than distributors)
- [ ] Securities are **represented upon issuance by a temporary global security** that is not exchangeable for definitive securities until expiry of the 40-day period AND, for persons other than distributors, until **certification of beneficial ownership** by a non-U.S. person or by a U.S. person who purchased in a transaction not requiring registration

**For equity securities:**

- [ ] Offers and sales prior to expiry of the applicable distribution compliance period are not made to a U.S. person or for U.S. account/benefit (other than distributors):
  - **1-year** compliance period for non-reporting issuers
  - **6-month** compliance period if the issuer is a reporting issuer

- [ ] During the distribution compliance period, each purchaser (other than a distributor) must certify **either** that:
  - [ ] It is not a U.S. person and is not acquiring for the account or benefit of any U.S. person; OR
  - [ ] It is a U.S. person who purchased in a transaction that did not require registration

- [ ] Each purchaser agrees to:
  - [ ] Resell only in accordance with Reg S, pursuant to registration, or pursuant to an available registration exemption
  - [ ] Not engage in hedging transactions with regard to such securities unless in compliance with the Securities Act

- [ ] For **domestic issuer** equity, the securities must contain a **legend** stating:
  - Transfer is prohibited except in accordance with Reg S, registration, or an available exemption
  - Hedging transactions involving the securities may not be conducted unless in compliance with the Act

- [ ] The issuer must, **by contract or governing document provision**, refuse to register any transfer not made in accordance with Reg S, registration, or an available exemption — or, if bearer form or foreign law prevents refusal, other reasonable procedures (such as a legend) must be implemented

> **Legal advisor note:** Category 3 equity for domestic issuers is the most burdensome structure. It applies to any U.S.-incorporated issuer raising offshore that does not qualify for Category 1 or 2 — most commonly private placements of domestic startup equity. The purchaser certification, securities-level legend, transfer-refusal mechanism, and hedging restriction all need to be operationalised through enforceable purchase-agreement covenants and transfer-agent procedures; the issuer's governing documents (charter / bylaws / LLC agreement) typically need amendment to support the Rule 903(b)(3)(iii)(B)(4) transfer-refusal duty.

**Reference:** 17 CFR § 230.903(b)(3)

---

### 3.3 — Special Rules: Guaranteed Securities and Warrants

#### Guaranteed Debt Securities (Rule 903(b)(4))

- [ ] For debt securities **fully and unconditionally guaranteed as to principal and interest by the parent** of the issuer: only the conditions applicable to the **guarantee** (based on the parent's category eligibility) must be satisfied for both the guaranteed debt securities and the guarantee

#### Warrants (Rule 903(b)(5)) — applies to Category 2 and 3

- [ ] Each warrant bears a **legend** stating:
  - The warrant and underlying securities are unregistered
  - The warrant may not be exercised by or on behalf of any U.S. person unless registered or exempt
- [ ] Each warrant exerciser provides **either**:
  - Written certification that the exerciser is not a U.S. person and is not exercising on behalf of a U.S. person; OR
  - Written legal opinion that the warrant and underlying securities are registered or exempt
- [ ] Procedures are in place to ensure the warrant **cannot be exercised within the United States** and securities cannot be **delivered within the United States** upon exercise, unless registered or exempt

**Reference:** 17 CFR § 230.903(b)(4)–(5)

## SECTION 4 — OFFSHORE RESALES BY NON-ISSUERS (Rule 904)

*Rule 904 covers offers and sales by persons other than the issuer, a distributor, their affiliates (except officers/directors who are affiliates only by position), or persons acting on their behalf. These are secondary market resales.*

---

### 4.1 — Universal Conditions for Offshore Resales (Rule 904(a))

All resales under Rule 904 must satisfy ALL THREE:

- [ ] **The offer and sale are made in an offshore transaction** (Rule 902(h))
- [ ] **No directed selling efforts are made in the United States** by the seller, any affiliate, or any person acting on their behalf
- [ ] **The applicable additional conditions** (Section 4.2 below), if applicable, are satisfied

---

### 4.2 — Additional Conditions

#### For Dealers and Persons Receiving Selling Concessions (Rule 904(b)(1))

Applies during the applicable distribution compliance period (Category 2 or 3):

- [ ] **Neither the seller nor any person acting on its behalf knows that the offeree or buyer is a U.S. person**
- [ ] If the seller or their agent knows the purchaser is a dealer or a selling-concession recipient:
  - [ ] Seller or their agent sends a **confirmation or other notice** stating that the securities may be offered and sold during the distribution compliance period only in accordance with Reg S, pursuant to registration, or pursuant to an available exemption

#### For Officer or Director Affiliates (Rule 904(b)(2))

Applies where the seller is an officer or director of the issuer or distributor, who is an affiliate **solely by virtue of holding such position** (not by reason of ownership or other affiliation):

- [ ] **No selling concession, fee, or other remuneration** is paid in connection with such offer or sale other than the **usual and customary broker's commission** that would be received by a person executing the transaction as agent

**Reference:** 17 CFR § 230.904

## SECTION 5 — RESALE LIMITATIONS FOR DOMESTIC ISSUER EQUITY (Rule 905)

*This is the "one-way valve" provision. Even if the initial offshore sale was clean under Reg S, equity of domestic issuers remains restricted.*

---

### 5.1 — Restricted Securities Status

- [ ] You understand that equity securities of **domestic issuers** acquired from the issuer, a distributor, or any of their affiliates in a Reg S transaction are deemed **"restricted securities"** under Rule 144 — exactly as if they had been sold in a private placement under Rule 506
- [ ] You understand that resales of such restricted securities by offshore purchasers must be made either:
  - In accordance with Regulation S (Rules 901–905)
  - Pursuant to registration under the Securities Act
  - Pursuant to an available exemption from registration (e.g., Rule 144 after applicable holding period)
- [ ] You understand that **Rule 901 and Rule 904 resales of domestic-issuer equity do not "wash" the restricted status** — the securities remain restricted even after a Reg S secondary transfer

> **Legal advisor note:** This is the sharpest edge in Regulation S for domestic issuers. Securities issued by a U.S.-incorporated entity remain restricted even after a clean offshore Reg S sale. Secondary purchasers who later try to sell into the U.S. without registration or an exemption — or who hold for less than Rule 144's one-year (non-reporting issuer) or six-month (reporting issuer) holding period — face Section 5 liability. The restricted status must be disclosed prominently to every offshore purchaser and enforced through transfer-agent procedures, share-certificate legends, or equivalent transfer restrictions.

**Reference:** 17 CFR § 230.905; 17 CFR § 230.144

**Cross-reference:** For the full Rule 144 resale conditions (holding period, current public information, volume limits, manner of sale, Form 144) that govern any U.S. resale of these restricted securities, see [rule-144-checklist.md](rule-144-checklist.md) — particularly §2.3 (restricted securities definition, including the Reg S Cat 3 bridge at (a)(3)(v)), §5 (holding period), §4 (current public information — the binding constraint for non-reporting issuers).

## SECTION 6 — OPERATIONAL & DOCUMENTATION REQUIREMENTS

*Regulation S is not merely a set of legal conditions — it requires operational infrastructure to enforce those conditions at the transaction level. This section covers the practical compliance build-out.*

---

### 6.1 — Pre-Offering Documentation

- [ ] **Offering memorandum / subscription agreement** contains:
  - [ ] Representations from each purchaser that it is not a U.S. person (or, for Category 3, that purchase did not require registration)
  - [ ] Agreement to resell only per Reg S, registration, or exemption
  - [ ] For domestic-issuer equity: agreement not to engage in hedging transactions unless in compliance with the Act
  - [ ] Acknowledgment that securities are restricted securities under Rule 144 (for domestic issuer equity)
  - [ ] Clear disclosure of the applicable distribution compliance period and restrictions on resale during that period
- [ ] **Distributor agreements** contain the required written agreement to comply with offering restrictions (Rule 902(g)(1))
- [ ] **Offering materials** (prospectus, offering circular, term sheet) contain the required Reg S legends on the cover page, in the underwriting section, and in all advertisements

---

### 6.2 — Legend Requirements

- [ ] **Offering documents** contain legend: *"The securities offered hereby have not been registered under the U.S. Securities Act of 1933, as amended, and may not be offered or sold in the United States or to U.S. persons (other than distributors) unless registered under the Act or an applicable exemption from registration is available."*
- [ ] **Domestic issuer equity** offering documents additionally contain hedging legend: *"Hedging transactions involving these securities may not be conducted unless in compliance with the U.S. Securities Act of 1933."*
- [ ] **Category 3 domestic issuer equity** securities themselves bear the required transfer-restriction legend on the certificate (or equivalent legend on the book-entry record / DRS statement where uncertificated)

---

### 6.3 — Investor Verification & KYC Controls

- [ ] **Purchaser eligibility verification** process established:
  - [ ] IP-based geofencing of any online offering portal or subscription page
  - [ ] Self-certification via purchaser questionnaire (non-U.S. person status, not purchasing for U.S. account or benefit)
  - [ ] KYC documentation reviewed and confirmed for each purchaser
  - [ ] U.S. persons screened out at the point of purchase, not merely notified post-purchase
- [ ] **Record retention** — Regulation S itself does not impose a record retention period on issuers (Rule 17a-4 retention applies only to broker-dealers). As a **practical recommendation** aligned with SEC investigation lookback windows and statute-of-limitations exposure, maintain records of all purchaser certifications, KYC documentation, and distributor confirmations for at least **5 years** from the offering date (longer if your home-jurisdiction AML rules require)
- [ ] **Designated offshore securities market** confirmed for any resales under Rule 904 that rely on execution through such a market

---

### 6.4 — Monitoring During the Distribution Compliance Period

- [ ] **Secondary market monitoring** — process in place to track known resales during the compliance period and confirm that none violate the restrictions
- [ ] **Distributor compliance monitoring** — process to confirm distributors have sent required confirmation notices to downstream dealers within the compliance period
- [ ] **U.S. market conditioning monitoring** — marketing team and PR team briefed on directed selling effort restrictions; all U.S.-facing press inquiries reviewed before response; social media accounts have geofencing or content restrictions where legally required

## SECTION 8 — INTERACTION WITH OTHER EXEMPTIONS & REGULATIONS

*Regulation S often works in tandem with other U.S. securities law exemptions. Identify cross-regulatory impacts before structuring.*

---

### 8.1 — Reg S + Reg D (Rule 506) Concurrent Offerings

- [ ] If conducting a **concurrent U.S. Reg D offering**, confirm that:
  - U.S. and offshore tranches are clearly separated
  - No "integration" of the two offerings (different offering terms, different investor pool, adequate time separation or structural separation)
  - Directed selling efforts from the Reg S tranche do not bleed into the U.S. market and endanger the Reg D exemption

### 8.2 — Reg S + Exchange Act Reporting

- [ ] If the issuer is a **reporting company** (Exchange Act §§ 12 or 15(d)):
  - Reg S securities counts affect §12(g) registered-holder thresholds (2,000 holders of record, or 500 non-accredited, plus $10M+ in assets — different thresholds apply to foreign private issuers)
  - Offshore purchasers who become **5% beneficial owners** of a §12-registered class trigger **§13(d)** Schedule 13D filing obligations (within 10 days; §13G available for passive investors)
  - **§16** obligations (Form 3/4/5 reporting and §16(b) short-swing profit recovery) apply to officers, directors, and **10% beneficial owners** of a §12-registered class
  - Form 20-F / Form 10-K disclosures may need to reference the offshore offering

### 8.3 — Reg S + Rule 144 (Resales)

- [ ] For **domestic-issuer equity** acquired in Reg S transactions, resales into the U.S. under Rule 144 require:
  - A minimum **one-year holding period** from the date of acquisition (non-reporting issuers), or **six months** (reporting issuers)
  - Compliance with all applicable Rule 144 volume, manner of sale, and current public information requirements
  - The Reg S offshore holding period does **not** count toward the Rule 144 holding period in any special way — it is measured from the date of acquisition

**Cross-reference:** Full Rule 144 conditions (non-affiliate vs affiliate paths, the 3-month ex-affiliate tail, tacking rules for conversions/gifts/trusts/holding-company formations, the shell-company carve-out for de-SPAC structures) are detailed in [rule-144-checklist.md](rule-144-checklist.md). Do not rely on the Reg S checklist alone for Rule 144 analysis.

### 8.4 — AML / BSA Compliance

- [ ] **Bank Secrecy Act (BSA) and FinCEN obligations** apply independently of Reg S:
  - If the offering involves money transmission, broker-dealer intermediaries must comply with FinCEN registration and AML program requirements
  - For digital asset offerings, assess whether the token or the platform constitutes a "money services business" under FinCEN guidance
  - OFAC sanctions screening required for all purchasers — Reg S does not create a sanctions exemption

**Cross-reference:** Full OFAC sanctions compliance framework (U.S.-person scope, the four screening dimensions including the SDN List and crypto wallet addresses, the 50 Percent Rule for beneficial-ownership look-through, comprehensive country sanctions, blocking and TD F 90-22.50 reporting within 10 business days, Annual Report of Blocked Property by September 30, General and Specific License frameworks, voluntary self-disclosure, virtual currency-specific guidance, recent crypto enforcement actions) is detailed in [ofac-checklist.md](ofac-checklist.md). OFAC applies on a strict-liability basis to every U.S. person in the transaction chain (issuer, placement agent, KYC provider, broker, transfer agent, smart contract operator); satisfying Reg S does not relieve any of these obligations.

## SECTION 9 — COMMON FAILURE MODES

*The following are the most frequent ways issuers inadvertently collapse the Regulation S safe harbor. Review each before launch.*

---

### 9.1 — Pre-Offering and Offering Phase

- [ ] **Conditioning the U.S. market** before offering commencement — social media posts, blog articles, podcast appearances, or press interviews referencing the offering that were accessible in the U.S.
- [ ] **Inadequate purchaser verification** — relying solely on purchaser self-certification without KYC, allowing U.S. persons to purchase through nominee arrangements
- [ ] **Failing to implement offering restrictions** — distributors not signing written agreements; offering materials missing required legends
- [ ] **Not sending dealer confirmation notices** — distributors selling to downstream dealers within the compliance period without sending the required restriction notice

### 9.2 — Distribution Compliance Period

- [ ] **Pre-maturity resales** — offshore purchasers reselling to U.S. persons before the distribution compliance period expires
- [ ] **Market conditioning during the offering** — issuer or affiliates issuing U.S. press releases or investor updates referencing the Reg S offering in a manner that conditions the U.S. market
- [ ] **Hedging transactions** — short selling or derivative positions taken against domestic-issuer equity before compliance period expiry

## SECTION 10 — ENFORCEMENT & PENALTIES

*Understanding the consequences of Reg S non-compliance is essential for appropriate compliance investment.*

---

### 10.1 — SEC Enforcement Exposure

- [ ] **Section 5 liability** — failure to satisfy Reg S conditions means the offering is unregistered and violates Section 5. Civil liability under Section 12(a)(1) gives purchasers the right to **rescission** (return of the purchase price plus interest) — potentially the full offering amount
- [ ] **SEC civil injunctive and administrative actions** — the SEC has jurisdiction over any securities offering that occurs in the U.S. or involves U.S. persons, regardless of issuer domicile
- [ ] **Disgorgement** — the SEC may require disgorging all proceeds from non-compliant Reg S offerings
- [ ] **Fraud liability** — Sections 17(a) and 10(b) apply independently of Reg S, regardless of offshore structure

### 10.2 — Criminal Exposure

- [ ] **DOJ prosecution** — willful violations of Section 5 are criminal offenses (15 U.S.C. § 77x): up to **5 years imprisonment** and/or monetary fines per individual violation
- [ ] **Wire fraud / securities fraud** — use of any means of interstate commerce to conduct a fraudulent offering carries separate federal criminal exposure

> **Legal advisor note:** The SEC has consistently held that economic substance — not legal form or contractual labels — determines whether a security is offered to U.S. persons. Leading cases on Reg S / Reg D integration and offshore-structuring challenges include *SEC v. Telegram Group Inc.* (S.D.N.Y. 2020) — enjoining a distribution on the ground that the offshore exempt sale and the subsequent public launch were a single integrated unregistered offering — and *SEC v. Kik Interactive Inc.* (S.D.N.Y. 2020), applying similar integration analysis. *SEC v. LBRY, Inc.* (D.N.H. 2022) further confirmed substance-over-form review. Securities sold to non-U.S. addresses that are subsequently transferred to U.S. holders before the compliance period expires — particularly through liquid secondary markets — may still constitute an unregistered U.S. offering.
