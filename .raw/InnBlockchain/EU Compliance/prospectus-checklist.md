# Prospectus Regulation Business Readiness Checklist
## The Legal Advisor's First-Meeting Guide for Issuers Raising Public Capital in the EU

**Prepared by InnBlockchain** | Smart Contract Engineering & Security Audit for Regulated FinTechs
**Regulation:** EU Prospectus Regulation — Regulation (EU) 2017/1129
**Amended by:** EU Listing Act — Regulation (EU) 2024/2809 (phased application: **4 December 2024 / 5 March 2026 / 5 June 2026**)
**Status:** In force since 21 July 2019; first tranche of Listing Act amendments in force since 4 December 2024; new prospectus formats from 5 March 2026; new threshold regime from 5 June 2026
**Document type:** Pre-engagement readiness assessment — to be completed before drafting begins

---

> **Who this document is for:** Founders, CFOs, finance directors, and executive teams preparing to raise public capital in the EU — whether through a traditional IPO, a public bond issuance, a secondary offering, or a tokenised-securities offering. This checklist mirrors the intake assessment a specialist EU securities lawyer would walk you through in a first advisory session.

---

> **How to use this checklist:** Work through each section in order. Every item marked with a checkbox (☐) represents a concrete requirement or decision you must resolve. Items you cannot check off represent gaps that must be closed before the prospectus is filed for approval. Your legal advisor will use your responses to scope the engagement, estimate the offer timetable, and identify deal-breakers early.

---

## SECTION 1 — DO YOU NEED A PROSPECTUS?

*This is the first question any legal advisor will ask. Not every capital-raising activity needs a prospectus — but almost every offer of transferable securities to the EU public does.*

---

### 1.1 — Trigger Test: Does the Prospectus Regulation Apply to You?

Answer each question. If any answer is **Yes**, the obligation in Article 3 is engaged unless an exemption applies.

- [ ] Are you offering **transferable securities** to the public in the EU (any communication, in any form, that presents sufficient information on the terms of the offer to enable an investor to decide to acquire) *(Art. 2(d))*?
- [ ] Are you seeking **admission of transferable securities to trading on a regulated market** situated or operating in an EU/EEA Member State *(Art. 3(3))*?
- [ ] Is your security a "transferable security" within MiFID II Art. 4(1)(44) — i.e. **negotiable on the capital market** (excluding pure instruments of payment)?

> **Legal advisor note:** The Prospectus Regulation borrows the *definition* of "transferable security" from MiFID II — but the *issuer* does not need MiFID II authorisation. MiFID authorisation hits the underwriters, placing agents, and trading venues. The issuer's only obligation is the prospectus itself. Don't conflate the two.

**Reference:** Art. 1 (scope), Art. 2(a) (definition of "securities"), Art. 3 (obligation to publish a prospectus), MiFID II Art. 4(1)(44) (transferable security)

---

### 1.2 — Exclusion Check: Is Your Security Outside the Regulation's Scope?

The Prospectus Regulation does **not** apply to the following carve-outs in Article 1(2). Check any that describe your security:

- [ ] **Units issued by collective investment undertakings other than closed-end type** (UCITS, open-ended AIFs) → regulated under UCITS / AIFMD, not Prospectus *(Art. 1(2)(a))*
- [ ] **Non-equity securities issued by Member States, regional/local authorities, or public international bodies** *(Art. 1(2)(b))*
- [ ] **Shares in the capital of central banks** *(Art. 1(2)(c))*
- [ ] **Securities unconditionally and irrevocably guaranteed by an EU Member State** *(Art. 1(2)(d))*
- [ ] **Securities issued by non-profit associations / charities** *(Art. 1(2)(e))*
- [ ] **Non-fungible / unique securities** — note: a token issued in a *large series indicating fungibility in substance* does not qualify as unique even if technically distinct (parallel to MiCA NFT analysis)

---

### 1.3 — Exemption Check: Is the *Offer* Exempt from the Prospectus Obligation?

Even if the Regulation applies, the obligation to publish a prospectus may be exempted. Check any that describe your offer:

#### Exemptions from the *public offer* obligation *(Art. 1(4) + Art. 3(2))*:

- [ ] **Threshold-based exemption (regime depends on filing date):**
  - **Until 4 June 2026:** offer < **€1 000 000** over 12 months → outside the Regulation entirely *(Art. 1(3))*; offers between €1M and the home Member State's chosen cap (up to **€8 000 000**) → MS opt-in exemption from the prospectus obligation, but MS may impose national disclosure document
  - **From 5 June 2026 (Listing Act):** Art. 1(3) deleted; single threshold of **€12 000 000** per issuer or offeror over 12 months, below which non-passported offers are exempt from the prospectus obligation *(amended Art. 3(2))*. Member States may alternatively opt for a **€5 000 000** threshold. MS may still impose a national disclosure document below the applicable threshold.
- [ ] **Offer solely to qualified investors** (MiFID II Annex II professionals + eligible counterparties) *(Art. 1(4)(a))*
- [ ] **Offer to fewer than 150 natural or legal persons per Member State**, other than qualified investors *(Art. 1(4)(b))*
- [ ] **Minimum denomination per unit ≥ €100 000** *(Art. 1(4)(c))*
- [ ] **Minimum total consideration per investor ≥ €100 000** *(Art. 1(4)(d))*
- [ ] **Offer of shares in substitution for existing shares of the same class** *(Art. 1(4)(e))*
- [ ] **Securities offered in connection with a takeover / merger / division** with an equivalent disclosure document *(Art. 1(4)(f)–(g))*
- [ ] **Dividends paid in shares of the same class** *(Art. 1(4)(h))*
- [ ] **Employee share schemes** — securities offered to current/former directors or employees by their employer / affiliated undertaking *(Art. 1(4)(i))*

#### Exemptions from the *admission to trading* obligation *(Art. 1(5))*:

- [ ] **Fungibility exemption** — admission of shares fungible with shares already admitted to trading on the same regulated market, representing **less than 30%** of the number of shares already admitted, over 12 months *(Art. 1(5)(a); raised from 20% to 30% by the Listing Act, effective 4 December 2024)*
- [ ] **Conversion / exchange shares** below the fungibility threshold *(Art. 1(5)(b))*
- [ ] **Shares resulting from exercise of options / convertibles** below threshold *(Art. 1(5)(c))*
- [ ] **Securities already admitted to trading on another EU regulated market** for ≥ 18 months on the same conditions *(Art. 1(5)(j))*
- [ ] **Takeover / merger / division consideration shares** with equivalent disclosure *(Art. 1(5)(f)–(g))*
- [ ] **Dividends paid in shares of the same class already admitted** *(Art. 1(5)(h))*
- [ ] **Employee share schemes** for securities already admitted *(Art. 1(5)(i))*

> **Legal advisor note:** Exemptions are construed narrowly. Marketing a "QI-only" offer that subsequently leaks to retail (through forwarded decks, social media, secondary trading) destroys the exemption. The same applies to the "<150 persons per Member State" test — counting is per offer, not per page-view. Document the counting methodology at offer launch.

**Reference:** Arts. 1(2)–(5), 3, 4, 5

---

### 1.4 — MiFID II / MiCA / Prospectus Boundary

For tokenised offerings, the classification decision determines which regime applies. These categories are **mutually exclusive**:

| Token type | Regime | Disclosure document |
|---|---|---|
| Ownership / debt / derivative exposure (equity tokens, security tokens, tokenised bonds) | **MiFID II + Prospectus** | Prospectus |
| Asset-referenced token (basket-pegged, no ownership) | **MiCA Title III (ART)** | MiCA white paper |
| E-money token (single-currency stablecoin) | **MiCA Title IV (EMT)** | MiCA white paper |
| Utility / access / governance (no ownership, no asset reference) | **MiCA Title II** | MiCA white paper |
| Unique NFT | **Out of both regimes** (subject to consumer law) | — |

- [ ] **Token classification opinion obtained** in writing from counsel
- [ ] **ESMA Article 2(5) MiCA guidelines** (on financial-instrument classification boundary) consulted against the current version
- [ ] **National NCA guidance** consulted — different NCAs (BaFin, AMF, AFM, CONSOB, CSSF) have published their own interpretive guidance on token classification

**Reference:** MiCA Art. 2(4)(a) (financial-instruments carve-out), MiCA Art. 2(5) (ESMA guidelines), MiFID II Art. 4(1)(44) (transferable security definition)

## SECTION 2 — WHAT KIND OF PROSPECTUS DO YOU NEED?

*The Listing Act (Reg 2024/2809) substantially restructured the prospectus formats available from 5 March 2026. Pick the format that matches your offer.*

---

### 2.1 — Format Decision Tree

#### ☐ FORMAT A — Standard Prospectus *(Art. 6 + Annexes via Delegated Reg (EU) 2019/980)*
*First-time public offer or admission, or any offer that does not qualify for a lighter format*

- Single document **or** separate documents (Registration Document + Securities Note + Summary)
- **Page limit (Listing Act): 300 pages** for offers of shares (excluding summary, information incorporated by reference, URD content, complex financial history disclosure)
- Standardised format and sequence mandatory (ESMA RTS)

#### ☐ FORMAT B — Base Prospectus *(Art. 8)*
*Non-equity securities issued under a programme or in a continuous / repeated manner*

- Base prospectus covers programme structure + general terms
- Issue-specific "**final terms**" filed for each individual issuance (no NCA approval required for final terms, only filing)
- Annual update required
- **Listing Act change:** new annual / interim financial information may be incorporated by reference rather than requiring a supplement

#### ☐ FORMAT C — Universal Registration Document (URD) *(Art. 9)*
*For frequent issuers — annual filing covering issuer's legal, business, financial, accounting and shareholding information*

- Filed annually; after **two consecutive years** of filing, subsequent URDs do **not** require prior NCA approval (filed-only regime)
- Becomes the "Registration Document" component for any prospectus the issuer subsequently uses
- Enables fast-track 5-working-day approval of subsequent securities-note + summary

#### ☐ FORMAT D — EU Follow-on Prospectus *(NEW under Listing Act, replaces Art. 14 simplified disclosure)*
*Secondary issuances by issuers with securities admitted to a regulated market or SME growth market for ≥ 18 months continuously*

- Available for both equity and non-equity
- Significantly reduced disclosure vs. standard prospectus
- Includes a simplified summary
- Standardised format and sequence
- Page limit for shares only
- Replaces the legacy "simplified prospectus for secondary issuances"
- Enables transition from SME growth market to regulated market

#### ☐ FORMAT E — EU Growth Issuance Prospectus *(NEW under Listing Act, replaces Art. 15 EU Growth Prospectus)*
*SMEs, non-SME issuers on SME growth markets, and small unlisted companies with offers ≤ €50 000 000 over 12 months*

- **NOT available** for issuers admitted to a regulated market (to avoid two-tier standards on the same venue)
- Single-document format
- Significantly reduced disclosure
- Alleviated summary
- Page limit for shares only
- Standardised format and sequence

> **Legal advisor note:** The Listing Act's new EU Follow-on Prospectus and EU Growth Issuance Prospectus apply from **5 March 2026**. Until that date, the legacy simplified disclosure regime (old Art. 14) and EU Growth Prospectus (old Art. 15) apply. The Listing Act's other prospectus changes apply on different dates: tranche 1 (fungibility exemption, supplement withdrawal-right extension, financial intermediary obligations) was already live from **4 December 2024**; tranche 3 (new exemption threshold regime under Art. 3(2)) applies from **5 June 2026**. Existing prospectuses approved before each new tranche applies enter transitional periods — check the transition rules with counsel for any filing window straddling these dates.

**Reference:** Arts. 6, 8, 9, 14 (legacy / replaced), 15 (legacy / replaced); Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2019/980 (annexes / schedules); Reg (EU) 2024/2809 (Listing Act)

## SECTION 3 — WHO IS RESPONSIBLE? (PERSONS RESPONSIBLE FOR THE PROSPECTUS)

*Civil liability attaches to specific named persons. Identifying them is a Day-1 task, not a drafting-stage afterthought.*

---

### 3.1 — Responsibility Statement *(Art. 11(1))*

- [ ] **Persons responsible identified** — at minimum: the issuer (or its administrative/management/supervisory body), the offeror, the person asking for admission to trading on a regulated market, **or** the guarantor, as applicable
- [ ] **Responsibility statement included in prospectus** — clear declaration that "to the best of the knowledge of [persons responsible], the information contained in the prospectus is in accordance with the facts and the prospectus makes no omission likely to affect its import"
- [ ] **Each responsible person clearly named** with their function and registered address
- [ ] **Internal sign-off process** documented — each responsible person has reviewed and approved the document in their capacity

### 3.2 — Civil Liability *(Art. 11(2))*

- [ ] **Awareness:** Member State law attaches **civil liability** to the persons responsible for the prospectus (and where applicable, the guarantor) for losses suffered by investors as a result of a misleading or incomplete prospectus
- [ ] **No liability attaches to the summary alone** unless the summary, read together with the rest of the prospectus, is misleading, inaccurate, or inconsistent, or fails to provide key information *(Art. 11(2))*
- [ ] **D&O insurance scope confirmed** — covers civil liability under the home Member State's prospectus regime
- [ ] **Underwriter / placing agent indemnities** drafted and agreed

> **Legal advisor note:** Liability allocation is fact-specific to the home Member State — Germany, France, the Netherlands, and Ireland have meaningfully different prospectus liability frameworks (negligence vs. strict, joint vs. several, statutory caps vs. uncapped). The forum-selection decision is often more material to the issuer's risk profile than the choice of underwriter.

**Reference:** Art. 11

## SECTION 4 — CONTENT REQUIREMENTS

*The prospectus must contain "the information necessary to enable investors to make an informed assessment" of the issuer and the securities. This is a substance test, not a checklist test — but the schedules give you the checklist.*

---

### 4.1 — Material Information Standard *(Art. 6)*

- [ ] **Information is necessary, material, and presented in an easily analysable, concise and comprehensible form** *(Art. 6(1)–(2))*
- [ ] **Information is tailored to** (i) the nature of the securities, (ii) the issuer's circumstances, (iii) the type of investors the offer targets, (iv) the type of trading venue the admission seeks
- [ ] **Plain-language summary** prepared (see Section 5)
- [ ] **Schedules / annexes** from Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2019/980 identified and completed for the issuer / security type combination

### 4.2 — Information Categories (per Annexes to Delegated Reg 2019/980)

- [ ] **Issuer information** — legal name, registered office, LEI, applicable law, group structure, history and development of the business
- [ ] **Business description** — principal activities, principal markets, competitive position, sources of revenue
- [ ] **Risk factors** — risks **specific to the issuer and/or to the securities** and **material** to an informed investment decision; assessed on **probability of occurrence × expected magnitude of negative impact**; presented in a **limited number of categories** depending on nature; **most material first** in each category; optional disclosure using a **qualitative scale of low / medium / high** *(Art. 16(1))*
- [ ] **Historical financial information** — typically 2–3 years of audited financial statements (subset depending on issuer type and format)
- [ ] **Operating and financial review** — narrative explanation of financial performance
- [ ] **Capital resources** — sources and amounts of cash, treatment of liquidity, capital structure
- [ ] **Management and governance** — directors, senior management, conflicts of interest, board practices, remuneration
- [ ] **Major shareholders** — control structure, related-party transactions
- [ ] **Terms of the offer / admission** — securities offered, offer price (or method of determination, see Art. 17 below), use of proceeds, expected timetable, dilution
- [ ] **Working capital statement** — issuer's working capital is sufficient for present requirements (or, if not, explanation)
- [ ] **Material contracts** — contracts material to the issuer's business, not entered into in the ordinary course
- [ ] **Documents on display** — list of documents available for inspection (typically constitutional documents, historical financials, fairness opinions)
- [ ] **Legal and arbitration proceedings** — material disclosures
- [ ] **Significant change statement** — no significant change since the date of the last published audited financial statements (or describe)

### 4.3 — Incorporation by Reference *(Art. 19)*

- [ ] **Documents to incorporate identified** — typically annual reports, half-year reports, regulatory disclosures, prior URDs / supplements
- [ ] **All incorporated documents are previously / simultaneously published electronically** and in a language accepted by the home NCA *(Art. 19(1))*
- [ ] **Cross-reference list** drafted — Article 19(2) requires a list identifying *where* in the source document each piece of incorporated information can be found
- [ ] **Incorporated information does NOT include the summary** — the summary cannot incorporate by reference

### 4.4 — Omission of Final Offer Price or Amount *(Art. 17)*

If you intend to launch before final price / amount is fixed:

- [ ] **Maximum offer price OR valuation method disclosed** in the prospectus, AND
- [ ] **Right of withdrawal** offered to investors after the final price / amount is filed with the NCA and published *(Art. 17(2))* — minimum window is **at least 2 working days** under the base text; verify with counsel whether the Listing Act tranche-1 amendments also extended this window to 3 working days (the Art. 23 supplement-withdrawal window was extended 2 → 3 effective 4 December 2024; confirm Art. 17 alignment)
- [ ] **Final price / amount filing** to NCA workflow ready

**Reference:** Arts. 6, 16, 17, 19; Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2019/980

## SECTION 5 — THE SUMMARY

*The summary is the only part of the prospectus most retail investors will ever read. Article 7 governs it, and Article 11(2) attaches liability to it where it misleads when read alongside the rest of the document.*

---

### 5.1 — Summary Content & Format *(Art. 7)*

- [ ] **Summary drafted** with the following four sections:
  - **(1) Introduction containing warnings** — securities denomination, ISIN, identity of issuer (name, address, LEI, contact), identity of competent authority, date of approval, standardised investor warning
  - **(2) Key information on the issuer** — who is the issuer, financial information, principal risks
  - **(3) Key information on the securities** — main features, where they will be traded, rights attached, dividends/distributions, principal risks
  - **(4) Key information on the offer / admission to trading** — terms and conditions, why is this prospectus being produced, use of proceeds, principal risks
- [ ] **Length:** maximum **7 sides of A4** when printed (Listing Act change: **+1 page per additional guarantor**)
- [ ] **Format:** clear, concise, easily analysable; **may include charts, graphs, and tables** post-Listing Act (within the page limit)
- [ ] **Language:** same accessibility standard as the rest of the prospectus; ESMA template / format (RTS) used
- [ ] **No incorporation by reference** in the summary
- [ ] **Consistency with the body** — every statement in the summary supportable by content elsewhere in the prospectus

> **Legal advisor note:** Article 11(2) attaches civil liability to the summary where it "misleads, is inaccurate, is inconsistent when read together with the other parts of the prospectus, or fails to provide key information." Drafting the summary last is malpractice — it must be drafted in parallel with the body and reviewed for consistency in every revision.

**Reference:** Art. 7, Art. 11(2); ESMA RTS on summary format

## SECTION 6 — APPROVAL PROCESS

*The NCA reviews the prospectus on three criteria: completeness, consistency, and comprehensibility (Art. 20). It is NOT a merit review.*

---

### 6.1 — NCA Approval Workflow *(Art. 20)*

| Step | Action | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Pre-submission meeting with NCA (optional but recommended for complex offerings, including tokenised securities) | T-2 to T-4 weeks |
| **2** | Initial filing with home NCA | T |
| **3** | NCA first round of review + comments | **10 working days** (standard) / **20 working days** (first-time issuer who has not previously had securities admitted) |
| **4** | Issuer response + revised draft submitted | Issuer-controlled timeline |
| **5** | Subsequent review rounds | **10 working days** per round |
| **6** | Approval granted, prospectus filed and published | Day of approval |

- [ ] **Home NCA identified** (home Member State per Art. 2(m))
- [ ] **Pre-submission engagement** considered, particularly for tokenised securities (where NCA guidance is evolving)
- [ ] **Q&A response capacity** in place — NCA rounds typically require 5–10 working day turnaround on issuer side
- [ ] **Realistic timetable** — 6–12 weeks from first filing to approval is typical for a straightforward IPO; complex / first-time / tokenised offerings often run 12–20 weeks

### 6.2 — Approval Standard *(Art. 20(4))*

The NCA's approval certifies that the prospectus meets:
- [ ] **Completeness** — all required information schedules covered
- [ ] **Consistency** — internally consistent across all sections (body, summary, financial statements)
- [ ] **Comprehensibility** — written in plain language, easily analysable

> **Legal advisor note:** NCA approval is NOT a statement that the issuer is a good investment, that the securities are suitable, or that the disclosure is *correct*. The issuer remains liable under Art. 11. Marketing the prospectus as "regulator-approved" is a compliance risk in itself.

**Reference:** Art. 20

---

### 6.3 — Publication *(Art. 21)*

- [ ] **Publication on issuer's website** in a dedicated, easily identifiable section, in electronic form, downloadable
- [ ] **Filing with NCA** for storage in the NCA's official mechanism
- [ ] **ESMA Storage Mechanism** — NCA forwards to ESMA centralised storage (no separate filing by issuer)
- [ ] **Availability for at least 10 years** from publication
- [ ] **Free of charge** to investors
- [ ] **Publication at least 6 working days before the close of the offer** (or as required by the offer-specific rules for IPO timing)

**Reference:** Art. 21

## SECTION 7 — ADVERTISEMENTS

*Every form of communication promoting the offer is an "advertisement" and is regulated. This includes investor decks, social media posts, press releases, podcast interviews, and tokenised-offer marketing pages.*

---

### 7.1 — Advertisement Rules *(Art. 22)*

- [ ] **Identifiable as an advertisement** — clearly marked as such
- [ ] **Reference to the prospectus** — states that a prospectus has been or will be published and where investors can obtain or will be able to obtain it
- [ ] **Fair, accurate, and not misleading**
- [ ] **Consistent with the prospectus** — no information in the advertisement contradicts or materially adds to information in the prospectus
- [ ] **Information disclosed orally or in writing** for advertising purposes is consistent with what is in the prospectus (Art. 22(4))
- [ ] **Advertisement review by the NCA** — the NCA has supervisory power over advertisements (and can require corrections)
- [ ] **Internal sign-off process** — marketing / IR cannot publish without compliance review
- [ ] **Social-media-specific protocol** — character-limited platforms still subject to "fair, accurate, not misleading" + prospectus reference; jurisdiction-blocking for non-public-offer Member States

> **Legal advisor note:** Article 22 obligations apply to **all** advertising, not just paid placements. A founder's tweet thread about the offer is an "advertisement" if it promotes the offer. NCAs increasingly monitor crypto-channel marketing for offers of security tokens.

**Reference:** Art. 22

---

## SECTION 8 — SUPPLEMENTS, VALIDITY & WITHDRAWAL RIGHTS

*The prospectus is alive for the duration of the offer. New material information triggers a supplement; supplements may give investors a withdrawal right.*

---

### 8.1 — Validity *(Art. 12)*

- [ ] **Validity period understood:** prospectus valid for **12 months** from approval, for offers to the public or admissions to trading on a regulated market, **provided** it is completed by any supplements required under Art. 23
- [ ] **Base prospectus validity:** 12 months from approval (with updates via incorporation by reference under Listing Act amendments)
- [ ] **URD validity:** 12 months from publication, with annual update

### 8.2 — Supplements *(Art. 23)*

- [ ] **Trigger awareness:** any **significant new factor, material mistake, or material inaccuracy** relating to the information in the prospectus that may affect the assessment of the securities, and arising between approval and the closing of the offer (or start of trading, whichever is later), requires a supplement
- [ ] **Approval workflow:** supplements must be approved by the NCA within **5 working days** of submission and published in the same way as the prospectus
- [ ] **Withdrawal right triggered** for investors who agreed to purchase or subscribe **before** the supplement was published — minimum **3 working days** to exercise (extended by the Listing Act from 2 working days, **permanently in force from 4 December 2024**; originally a temporary 2021 Covid-era measure that had expired)
- [ ] **Financial intermediary obligation** — when a supplement is published, the intermediary must contact (by **electronic means**, post-Listing Act) investors who purchased through it about their withdrawal right, by the end of the first working day after publication
- [ ] **Listing Act restriction:** a supplement to a **base prospectus cannot introduce a new type of security** not already covered by the base prospectus (limited exception where required by capital-requirements law)
- [ ] **Workflow for triggering events** documented — finance, legal, IR, and product teams know which events trigger supplement obligations (material litigation, change of control, restatements, profit warnings, material acquisitions/disposals)

> **Legal advisor note:** Supplement obligations often catch issuers off guard during the marketing period. A planned acquisition that closes mid-bookbuild, a regulatory inquiry that becomes public, or a downward earnings guidance revision can all force a supplement — which can delay the offer or trigger withdrawal rights that destroy the bookbuild. Plan for this risk.

**Reference:** Arts. 12, 23

## SECTION 9 — CROSS-BORDER PASSPORTING & LANGUAGES

*One of the regulation's two purposes is the single-disclosure passport — approval in one Member State is recognised in all 27.*

---

### 9.1 — Passporting Mechanism *(Arts. 24–25)*

- [ ] **Host Member States identified** — every Member State where the offer will be made publicly OR admission to trading sought
- [ ] **Notification request submitted** to home NCA, identifying each host Member State
- [ ] **Home NCA forwards "certificate of approval"** + copy of the prospectus to each host NCA via the **ESMA notification portal** within **1 working day** of the issuer's request (or, if requested with the application, within 1 working day of approval) *(Art. 25(1) and (6))*
- [ ] **Host NCA cannot undertake any approval or administrative procedure** beyond the home approval *(Art. 24(1))*
- [ ] **No notification fee** — Art. 25(5) prohibits any fee for the notification or receipt of notification of a prospectus, supplement, or related supervisory activity, in either the home or host Member State. (Approval fees in the home Member State under Art. 20(10) are separate and permitted.)

### 9.2 — Languages *(Art. 27)*

- [ ] **Home Member State language** — prospectus in a language accepted by the home NCA (typically the official language(s) of that Member State, often plus English)
- [ ] **Host Member State summary translation** — the **summary** must be translated into an official language of each host Member State (or another language accepted by the host NCA); the full prospectus may be in a language "customary in the sphere of international finance" — i.e. English, in most cases
- [ ] **Translator engagement contracted** in advance — summary translations into 5+ Member States typically need 2–3 weeks lead time

**Reference:** Arts. 24, 25, 27

## SECTION 10 — THIRD-COUNTRY ISSUERS

*Non-EU issuers can access the EU public market via two routes: an EU-approved prospectus under Art. 28, or recognition of a home-country prospectus under the equivalence regime in Art. 29.*

---

### 10.1 — Voluntary EU Prospectus *(Art. 28)*

- [ ] **Third-country issuer** elects to prepare a prospectus under the EU Regulation
- [ ] **Home NCA designation** — third-country issuer chooses a home Member State per Art. 2(m)(iii)
- [ ] **Full compliance with EU prospectus content and approval** rules
- [ ] **Benefit:** EU-wide passporting via Arts. 24–25

### 10.2 — Equivalence Route *(Art. 29)*

- [ ] **Equivalence decision in force** by the European Commission for the issuer's home country regime (only a handful of regimes have ever been declared equivalent — confirm current status)
- [ ] **Home-country prospectus drawn up under equivalent rules**, supplemented as needed
- [ ] **Cooperation arrangements between the home NCA and third-country regulator** in place (Art. 30)

> **Legal advisor note:** Equivalence decisions under Art. 29 are rare and slow. Most third-country issuers (US, UK, Swiss, Singaporean) use the Art. 28 route — i.e. prepare a fresh EU-compliant prospectus — rather than waiting for equivalence. Confirm the current state of equivalence decisions with counsel before assuming the Art. 29 route is available.

**Reference:** Arts. 28, 29, 30

## SECTION 11 — TOKENISED SECURITIES: SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS

*The Prospectus Regulation applies to tokenised securities exactly as it applies to traditional securities — the offer document is the same regime, even where the issuance and settlement happen on a DLT.*

---

### 11.1 — Token Classification (Pre-Prospectus Decision)

- [ ] **MiFID II Art. 4(1)(44) test applied** — is the token "negotiable on the capital market" (transferable security)?
  - Tokens conferring **ownership** (equity-like) → security
  - Tokens conferring **debt** (bond-like, repayment obligation) → security
  - Tokens conferring **derivative exposure** to an asset → security
  - **Yield without ownership** can still be security-classified depending on rights and structure (case-by-case)
- [ ] **MiCA Art. 2(4)(a) carve-out triggered** if the token is a MiFID financial instrument → MiCA does not apply
- [ ] **ESMA Article 2(5) MiCA classification guidelines** consulted (current version)
- [ ] **National NCA guidance** consulted — BaFin, AMF, AFM, CSSF, CONSOB, CNMV all have token-classification notes

### 11.2 — Prospectus Drafting Specifics for Tokenised Offerings

- [ ] **Technology description in risk factors and securities description**:
  - DLT / blockchain network used (permissioned vs. permissionless, consensus mechanism)
  - Smart contract address(es) and audit history
  - Token standard (ERC-3643, ERC-1400, or equivalent)
  - On-chain identity / KYC mechanism
  - Upgrade mechanism (proxy contracts, multisig governance)
  - Recovery procedures for lost private keys, frozen accounts, contract bugs
- [ ] **Smart contract audit report** completed by an independent third party with all material findings remediated, referenced in the prospectus
- [ ] **Custody disclosure** — how holders' tokens are held (self-custody, qualified custodian, hybrid), and what happens on custodian failure
- [ ] **Settlement and transfer mechanics** — how the token transfers post-issuance, whether transfers are restricted to whitelisted addresses
- [ ] **Use of DLT for issuance/settlement** disclosed, with reference to whether the firm is operating or using a DLT Pilot Regime venue
- [ ] **Tax disclosure** — token-specific tax treatment in each offer jurisdiction
- [ ] **Operational risk disclosure** — smart contract risk, oracle risk, bridge risk (if applicable), network congestion risk

### 11.3 — DLT Pilot Regime Interaction

- [ ] **Awareness:** the DLT Pilot Regime (Reg (EU) 2022/858) governs the trading and settlement *infrastructure* (DLT MTF / DLT SS / DLT TSS) — it does **not** waive the Prospectus Regulation disclosure obligation on the issuer
- [ ] **If listing on a DLT Pilot venue:** the venue operator will require a compliant prospectus before admitting the token; pre-listing engagement with the venue and its NCA recommended
- [ ] **DLT Pilot value caps** modelled — caps on aggregate market value per instrument and per venue may constrain offer size

### 11.4 — Common Tokenised-Offer Misframes

> **Misframe 1:** "We're issuing a security token, so we can use the MiCA white paper format." **Wrong.** MiCA Art. 2(4)(a) excludes MiFID financial instruments from MiCA. Security tokens need a prospectus, not a white paper.

> **Misframe 2:** "Our token is on a DLT Pilot MTF so we don't need a prospectus." **Wrong.** DLT Pilot is an infrastructure regime; it does not replace the issuer's disclosure obligation.

> **Misframe 3:** "Our utility token also gives revenue-share rights but it's a 'utility token' so MiCA applies." **Wrong if the revenue share is structured as ownership / claim.** Revenue rights without ownership can be MiCA-side; revenue rights *that confer enforceable claims on the issuer* tip into MiFID transferable-security territory. Get a written classification opinion.

**Reference:** MiFID II Art. 4(1)(44); MiCA Arts. 2(4)(a) and 2(5); Reg (EU) 2022/858 (DLT Pilot); ESMA classification guidelines (consult current version)

## SECTION 13 — LISTING ACT (Reg 2024/2809) TRANSITION

*The Listing Act is the most material amendment to the Prospectus Regulation since its adoption. Application is phased over three dates: 4 December 2024 / 5 March 2026 / 5 June 2026. Founders preparing offers that straddle any of these boundaries must know which regime applies on the filing date.*

---

### 13.1 — Listing Act Changes by Effective Date

#### Tranche 1 — Effective **4 December 2024** (already in force)

| Area | Pre-Listing Act | Post-Listing Act |
|---|---|---|
| **Fungibility exemption** *(Art. 1(5)(a))* | < 20% over 12 months | < **30%** over 12 months |
| **Supplement withdrawal right** *(Art. 23)* | 2 working days | **3 working days** (made permanent — was a 2021 temporary measure that had expired) |
| **Supplement contact obligation** *(Art. 23)* | Various | Financial intermediary must contact investors **by electronic means** by end of first working day after publication |

#### Tranche 2 — Effective **5 March 2026**

| Area | Pre-Listing Act | Post-Listing Act |
|---|---|---|
| **Secondary issuance format** *(replaces Art. 14)* | Simplified prospectus | **EU Follow-on Prospectus** (new format) |
| **SME format** *(replaces Art. 15)* | EU Growth Prospectus | **EU Growth Issuance Prospectus** (new format) |
| **Page limits** | None | **300 pages** (standard, shares only), separate limits for EU Follow-on / EU Growth Issuance Prospectus — excluding summary, IBR, URD, complex disclosures |
| **Standardised format** | Recommended | **Mandatory** (ESMA RTS templates) |
| **Summary** | Max 7 sides A4, no charts | Max 7 sides A4 + 1 page per additional guarantor, **charts / graphs / tables allowed** |
| **Base prospectus updates** | Supplement required | Annual / interim financial information **incorporable by reference** without supplement |
| **Supplements to base prospectus** | Could introduce new security types | **Cannot introduce new security types** (limited capital-requirements exception) |
| **SME offer cap (EU Growth Issuance Prospectus eligibility)** | €20M | **€50M** for small unlisted companies |

#### Tranche 3 — Effective **5 June 2026**

| Area | Pre-Listing Act | Post-Listing Act |
|---|---|---|
| **Outside-scope floor** *(Art. 1(3))* | < €1M over 12 months → outside Regulation entirely | **Article 1(3) deleted** — replaced by a unified exemption-from-obligation regime in amended Art. 3(2) |
| **Single exemption threshold** *(amended Art. 3(2))* | MS opt-in cap up to €8M (above €1M floor) | Single threshold of **€12M** per issuer/offeror over 12 months below which non-passported offers are exempt from the prospectus obligation; Member States may opt for **€5M** instead |

### 13.2 — Transition Workflow

- [ ] **Filing date mapped against each tranche** — tranche 1 changes are already live; tranche 2 changes apply only to prospectuses approved on or after 5 March 2026; tranche 3 changes apply only to offers commenced on or after 5 June 2026
- [ ] **Transitional provisions for legacy formats** — existing prospectuses approved under the old simplified prospectus or EU Growth Prospectus regimes carry their pre-existing validity; confirm grandfathering scope with counsel for any document with cross-tranche application
- [ ] **ESMA Level 2 RTS / Commission Delegated Regulations** — the standardised format, page-limit detail, and template layout sit in Commission Delegated Regulations adopted under the Listing Act; track adoption status before drafting begins
- [ ] **Drafting team trained** on the post-Listing Act formats and the live tranche-1 changes already in force

**Reference:** Regulation (EU) 2024/2809 — particularly the amending articles to Reg (EU) 2017/1129; ESMA RTS on format / templates / page limits

## SECTION 14 — PENALTIES & ENFORCEMENT

*Member States must provide for effective, proportionate, and dissuasive penalties. The floors below are minimum maxima — national law can go higher.*

---

### 14.1 — Administrative Penalty Exposure *(Art. 38)*

Article 38(1)(a) lists the infringements that must trigger at least the Art. 38(2) sanctions: **Article 3, Article 5, Article 6, Article 7(1) to (11), Article 8, Article 9, Article 10, Article 11(1) and (3), Article 14(1) and (2), Article 15(1), Article 16(1), (2) and (3), Article 17, Article 18, Article 19(1) to (3), Article 20(1), Article 21(1) to (4) and (7) to (11), Article 22(2) to (5), Article 23(1), (2), (3) and (5), and Article 27**. Article 38(1)(b) extends the same regime to failure to cooperate with an investigation or inspection under Article 32.

Article 38(2) requires Member States to ensure NCAs have at least the following five sanctions (national law may go higher per Art. 38(3)):

| Art. 38(2) | Sanction | Quantum |
|---|---|---|
| **(a)** | Public statement indicating the responsible person and the nature of the infringement (published in accordance with Art. 42) | — |
| **(b)** | Cease-and-desist order against the responsible natural or legal person | — |
| **(c)** | Profit disgorgement — at least **2× the amount of profits gained or losses avoided** because of the infringement, where determinable | — |
| **(d)** | Maximum administrative pecuniary sanction — **legal person** | At least **EUR 5 000 000** *or* **3% of total annual turnover** (whichever higher; consolidated turnover where applicable) |
| **(e)** | Maximum administrative pecuniary sanction — **natural person** | At least **EUR 700 000** |

- [ ] **Maximum-exposure quantum understood at executive level** and reflected in risk appetite + D&O insurance scope
- [ ] **Awareness:** decisions imposing penalties are **published** on the NCA's website with identity of the person sanctioned (subject to limited anonymisation, deferral, or non-publication grounds) *(Art. 42 — publication of decisions)*
- [ ] **Whistleblower / reporting-of-infringements mechanism** in place *(Art. 41 — reporting of infringements)*
- [ ] **Awareness:** the Art. 38(2) list is the **floor**, not the ceiling — Member States may provide additional sanctions, including a temporary ban from management functions or withdrawal/suspension of approval, under national law (Art. 38(3)); check the home Member State's transposition for the actual menu

### 14.2 — Civil Liability *(Art. 11)*

- [ ] **Civil liability exposure modelled** — under home Member State law, investors who have suffered loss as a result of an incomplete / misleading prospectus can bring damages claims against the persons responsible
- [ ] **D&O insurance scope confirmed** — covers prospectus civil liability in the home Member State and all passporting jurisdictions

**Reference:** Arts. 11 (civil liability), 38 (administrative sanctions and other administrative measures), 39 (exercise of supervisory powers), 40 (right of appeal), 41 (reporting of infringements / whistleblower), 42 (publication of decisions)
