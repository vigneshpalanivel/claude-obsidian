# 🎯 ICP — Trioangle Agencies & Consulting Firms (Channel Partner ICP)

> [!NOTE] Executive Summary
> Trioangle's Ideal Channel Partner is a **Consulting Firm, Digital Agency, or IT Services Company** that regularly receives client briefs for web and mobile applications—marketplace platforms, on-demand services, rental apps, mobility solutions, e-learning portals, and more—but lacks the in-house engineering bandwidth or domain-specific expertise to fulfill them profitably. These partners don't just "bring a project"; they bring a recurring pipeline of builds. By sub-contracting development to Trioangle, they expand their delivery capability without hiring more engineers. Trioangle becomes the silent build arm—the agency owns the client relationship; Trioangle delivers the product.
>
> **Pitch angle**: Trioangle is positioned as a **web, mobile, and AI engineering partner** with a decade of marketplace and on-demand platform delivery experience. Agencies sub-contract client builds—end-to-end, phase-specific (backend, mobile, QA), or AI-feature add-ons to existing platforms—and retain full ownership of the client relationship and billing.
>
> **Delivery modes — three service lines:**
> - **🤖 AI Engineering (Service line, post-pivot priority)** — AI features added to existing platforms or new AI-native builds: dynamic pricing, dispatch optimization, demand forecasting, RAG/agent flows, AI moderation, conversational interfaces ($15k–$120k+; AI-Forward Platform Operator profile; 4–8 week packages, often recurring as 2–4 projects/year per end-client). This is the **wedge that doesn't commoditize against vibe-coding tools**. Entry product is the **AI Audit Sprint** (see below), not generic Discovery.
> - **⚙️ Greenfield Custom Development (Service line)** — bespoke platforms for funded startups and enterprises ($10k–$120k+; full IP ownership; dedicated team; 2–6 month build timeline).
> - **🛒 Clone Scripts (Product line)** — pre-built, battle-tested apps for on-demand, mobility, rental, marketplace, classifieds, social, and e-learning verticals ($500–$25k; fast deployment; no custom architecture required). Primary route for P1 Overflow Agency partners whose end clients skew Product ICP.
>
> Partners route client briefs across these three lines based on brief type (AI feature vs. greenfield build vs. template-fit clone), budget, and scope complexity.
>
> **Paid scoping engagements:**
> - **🤖 AI Audit Sprint ($1.5k–$4k, 1–2 weeks)** — entry product for AI-feature briefs. Reviews the end-client's existing platform/data, identifies AI opportunity, produces feasibility scope + cost + ROI projection. Required pre-step for AI Engineering line.
> - **⚙️ Discovery Sprint ($1.5k–$4k, 1–2 weeks)** — generic scoping for Custom Dev briefs. Produces requirements spec, architecture blueprint, fixed-price delivery estimate.
> - Both can be passed through to the end client or absorbed in pre-sales.

## 😣 Pain Points

### Pain Point 1 — "We Win the Deal But Can't Deliver"
**Severity:** 10/10 | **Frequency:** Recurring — every few months when a large app project is won | **Category:** Functional + Financial + Reputational

- **Workaround:** Attempting to bridge the gap with AI coding tools (Cursor, Lovable, Copilot) using in-house developers — now the first attempt before escalating; when that stalls, reverting to random Upwork developers or panicking into hiring a local developer at a loss
- **Cost of Inaction:** Declining or botching a $15k–$120k client project = direct revenue loss + client relationship damage + reputational risk
- **Emotional Impact:** Deep anxiety about over-promising; fear of exposing the agency's delivery gaps; stress about margin erosion from expensive last-minute hires

> [!QUOTE] *"We're brilliant at client relationships and strategy, but every time they ask us to build an app, we either say no or scramble to stitch together freelancers who miss deadlines."*

---

### Pain Point 2 — "Our Offshore Partner Has No Domain Expertise"
**Severity:** 8/10 | **Frequency:** Per project when using a generalist offshore partner | **Category:** Functional + Process

- **Workaround:** Over-managing the development team, re-scoping features, reducing deliverables to hide the gap
- **Cost of Inaction:** Delivered product doesn't match client's vertical needs (e.g., a food delivery app that doesn't handle restaurant commission logic correctly); end client complains, agency reputation damaged
- **Emotional Impact:** Embarrassment vis-à-vis the client, loss of trust, potential refund demands

> [!QUOTE] *"Our dev partner in Eastern Europe builds generic apps. But our client's on-demand logistics platform has very specific dispatching and multi-zone pricing logic that they just couldn't handle."*

---

### Pain Point 3 — "We Can't Scale Our Service Line Without Hiring"
**Severity:** 8/10 | **Frequency:** Constant growth constraint | **Category:** Process + Financial

- **Workaround:** Staying small, rejecting large project opportunities, or burning out the 2–3 in-house developers
- **Cost of Inaction:** Agency revenue plateaus; competitors who have reliable delivery partners grow faster
- **Emotional Impact:** Frustration at the growth ceiling; resentment of having to turn away revenue

> [!QUOTE] *"We have the clients. We have the briefs. We just don't have the engineers for 3 simultaneous builds."*

---

### Pain Point 4 — "We Quote Blind and Either Lose the Deal or Win at a Loss"
**Severity:** 7/10 | **Frequency:** Every mid-to-large client project pitch | **Category:** Financial + Process

- **Workaround:** Over-scoping to protect margin (loses the bid on price) or under-scoping to win (destroys margin on delivery)
- **Cost of Inaction:** A mispriced $40k project can consume 60–70% margin in unplanned scope — the agency works the project at a loss while damaging the client relationship
- **Emotional Impact:** Financial anxiety about committing to a client price without a reliable technical estimate; fear of winning the deal and immediately regretting it

> [!QUOTE] *"We won a $45k app project and handed it to our dev partner. By the time they flagged the out-of-scope items, we'd already invoiced the client. We ate $18k."*

---

### Pain Point 5 — "We Burn Weeks on Proposals That Don't Convert"
**Severity:** 6/10 | **Frequency:** Every early-stage client pitch | **Category:** Process + Sales Efficiency

- **Workaround:** Spending 2–3 weeks on proposal decks before any revenue is confirmed — principal time that could be billed elsewhere
- **Cost of Inaction:** A 50% proposal close rate means half of all pre-sales investment is pure sunk cost; at 3 weeks per proposal, that's 6+ weeks of principal time wasted per quarter
- **Emotional Impact:** Exhaustion and resentment from pouring effort into technical proposals only to lose to a cheaper competitor or a client who "decided to wait"

> [!QUOTE] *"I spent 3 weeks on a proposal for a $60k app build. The client took our architecture doc, sent it to a cheaper agency, and ghosted us. That 3 weeks cost us two other opportunities we didn't have time to pursue."*

---

### Pain Point 6 — "If the Sub-Contractor Messes Up, We're the Ones Who Get Sued"
**Severity:** 7/10 | **Frequency:** Background anxiety on every sub-contracted project | **Category:** Legal + Reputational

- **Workaround:** Adding penalty clauses to sub-contractor agreements, taking out professional indemnity insurance, avoiding sub-contracting for high-stakes clients
- **Cost of Inaction:** A data breach, buggy payment module, or IP dispute exposes the agency to client litigation — even when the fault lies entirely with the sub-contractor; the agency is the party with the client contract
- **Emotional Impact:** Persistent background anxiety about legal exposure; reluctance to take on large projects without ironclad sub-contractor contracts they don't know how to write

> [!QUOTE] *"Our sub-contractor shipped a broken payment module. The client didn't sue them — they sued us. We had no contract clause to fall back on and had to refund $30k out of pocket."*

Partner intermediaries collapse the timeline dramatically:
>
> - **Digital Transformation Enterprise Buyer** ([[ICP - Service]] Persona 3) — Engages direct vendors primarily through formal RFP processes initiated by management consulting and Gartner/analyst relationships. Direct Clutch or LinkedIn outreach rarely clears the procurement bar on its own. A consulting firm already trusted by this buyer is the fastest path to the engagement.
> - **MENA Relationship Buyer** ([[ICP - Service]] Persona 4) — Relationship-first. A warm introduction from a MENA-based consulting firm outweighs cold portfolio evidence by a wide margin. UAE/KSA-based agency and consulting partners are the highest-leverage access channel for this segment.

## 🎤 Partner Discovery Interview Guide

> Target: 10–15 conversations. Mix: 5–7 existing Trioangle clients who run agencies, 3–5 agencies who declined or went elsewhere, 2–3 active warm prospects. 30 min calls.

> **Pre-call screening (before booking the interview):** Confirm the agency (a) regularly receives client briefs for app or platform builds, and (b) is open to sub-contracting development work. If either answer is no, skip the interview — the ICP doesn't fit.

### Context (5 min)
1. Tell me about your agency — what do you sell and who are your typical clients?
2. How does your team handle app or platform development today?
3. Walk me through how you typically handle a brief that requires more development bandwidth than you have in-house.

### Problem (10 min)
4. Walk me through the last time a client asked you to build an app or platform. What happened?
5. What's the most stressful part of delivering a development project you didn't fully staff for?
6. How often do you turn down projects because you don't have the build capacity?
7. What went wrong with the last offshore dev partner you used — or why haven't you used one?
8. What does it cost you (time, money, client trust) when a development delivery goes wrong?

### Partner Evaluation (10 min)
9. What would make you trust a new dev sub-contractor enough to put them on a live client project?
10. What would make you walk away from a potential dev partner immediately?
11. How do you currently scope and price development projects for clients? How painful is that?
12. What does your ideal sub-contracting arrangement look like — how much involvement do you want from the partner?

### Buying (5 min)
13. How did you find the last vendor or sub-contractor you brought on?
14. Who else in your firm would be involved in approving a dev partner?
15. What's the last hourly rate or project rate you paid a dev sub-contractor? What margin did you make on that engagement — and was it worth it?

### Close
- Anything else about how you manage dev delivery that I haven't asked about?
- Know any other agency founders or consulting principals who'd be willing to share their perspective?
- Would you be open to a 2-week pilot with a small live brief to experience our process firsthand?
